Featured project · Science communication

The Exhausting Side of Flexibility

Remote work and flexible schedules are sold as freedom. The research tells a more complicated story. A visual essay on why we can feel more autonomous and more depleted at the same time.

Freedom, balance, autonomy

Remote work and flexible schedules are widely marketed as key solutions for improving quality of life. Recent research reveals a far more nuanced reality.

So why are we still exhausted?

Despite more flexible conditions, numerous studies report rising stress, mental fatigue, and an inability to "switch off."

Work that's hard to leave behind

Constant notifications, late-night emails, and blurring boundaries between work and personal life can lead to psychological burnout.

Autonomy, but higher invisible demands

Flexibility can foster autonomy and job satisfaction, yet also bring more interruptions, difficulty disconnecting, and mental overload. Its effects often depend on the context in which it is implemented.

Specific populations face greater risk

Research suggests the negative effects of flexibility can be more pronounced for women, particularly when personal and professional responsibilities overlap.

The role of support

Flexibility is most beneficial when paired with organizational support, clear boundaries, and a healthy work environment. Flexibility alone is not enough.

So, does being more flexible truly mean being freer?

When poorly managed, flexibility blurs boundaries and increases mental load. Flexibility is not the problem in itself — the way it's designed and supported is what makes the difference.
